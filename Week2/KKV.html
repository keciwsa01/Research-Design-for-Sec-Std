🔬 Designing Social Inquiry

Scientific Inference in Qualitative Research - King, Keohane & Verba (1994)

🔬 Chapter 1: The Science in Social Science

🎯 The Central Challenge

"Social science has been split into two camps for decades. On one side, you have the 'quants' with their numbers and statistics. On the other, the 'quals' with their deep case studies and rich descriptions. But what if this divide is actually holding us back?"

Authors: Gary King (Harvard), Robert Keohane (Princeton), Sidney Verba (Harvard)

Published: 1994 - Princeton University Press

KKV's Revolutionary Argument

The divide is artificial! Both quantitative and qualitative research share the same logic of scientific inference. Although they use different tools, both aim to move from what we can observe to what we can infer about the world.

The Goal: Help qualitative researchers apply the same rigorous standards that make quantitative research powerful, without losing what makes qualitative research valuable.

Understanding the Two Styles

📊 Quantitative Studies

  • Tools: Numbers, statistics, large samples
  • Goal: Find general patterns across many cases
  • Strengths: Statistical rigor, replicable
  • Example: Survey of 1000 voters on election preferences

📚 Qualitative Studies

  • Tools: Cases, interviews, documents
  • Goal: Uncover detailed processes in depth
  • Strengths: Rich context, deep understanding
  • Example: In-depth study of one community's politics

Key Insight: Despite these differences, both must follow the same rules: use evidence to make valid inferences, describe uncertainty, and explain methods openly.

🌟 Success Stories

Lisa Martin's Economic Sanctions Study: Combined statistical analysis with detailed case studies to understand when sanctions work.

Robert Putnam's Italian Regional Governments: Used both quantitative measures and qualitative insights to explain why some regions govern better than others.

Four Features of Scientific Research

1Inference

Going beyond describing facts to explaining why or how. Not just "what happened" but "why it happened" or "what it means."

Example: Not just counting campaign ads, but inferring their effect on voter turnout.

2Public Procedures

Making methods clear so others can evaluate or replicate. Show exactly how research was done.

Why: Transparency lets others evaluate, replicate, or improve the work.

3Acknowledged Uncertainty

Reporting how confident or uncertain findings are. Admitting that conclusions are not perfect.

Why: Builds honesty and credibility - uncertainty doesn't weaken science, it strengthens it.

4Method Over Topic

Any subject can be studied scientifically if the process is systematic. Science is about how we study, not what we study.

Insight: Even emotional or cultural topics can be studied scientifically.

Science and Complexity

Social life is complex, but complexity does not make science impossible. Instead, researchers should use clear concepts, comparisons, and counterfactuals (what would have happened otherwise) to find patterns and test explanations.

The Goal: Good theory simplifies without oversimplifying.

Components of Research Design

KKV identify four major parts of every research project:

1Research Question

Must be both important and answerable. Should connect real-world importance with scholarly relevance.

2Theory

Should be precise, falsifiable, and produce observable implications. Must be testable and open to being proven wrong.

3Data

Must be valid, reliable, and well-documented. High-quality data are transparent and replicable.

4Use of Data

Involves analyzing and reporting results clearly. Must avoid bias and test multiple predictions.

Key Point: Research design is an iterative process — you refine your question, theory, and data as you learn more.

🧩 The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

The Impossible Dream

"Here's the thing that keeps social scientists up at night: We want to know if X causes Y. But to really prove it, we'd need to see the same exact situation both with X and without X. That's literally impossible."

🔄 The Counterfactual Problem

To prove causation, we need to observe the counterfactual — what would have happened if everything else stayed exactly the same, but our cause was absent.

🗳️ Example: Does Democracy Cause Peace?

What we observe: Germany is democratic and peaceful today.

What we need but can't see: The exact same Germany, with the same history, culture, economy, and leaders, but somehow not democratic.

The problem: We can't rewind history and run the experiment again!

Why This Matters

This isn't just a philosophical puzzle — it's the core challenge of all social science research. Every time we ask:

  • "Did this policy reduce crime?"
  • "Does education increase income?"
  • "Do international organizations prevent war?"

We're bumping up against the fundamental problem of causal inference.

The Solution Strategy

Since we can't observe the counterfactual directly, we have to get creative:

  • Find similar cases: Look for situations that are as close as possible to identical
  • Use variation: Study cases where the cause is present and absent
  • Control for differences: Account for other factors that might matter
  • Be explicit about assumptions: Acknowledge what we're assuming to make causal claims

📏 Five Rules for Strong Theories

Building Better Explanations

"A theory isn't just a hunch or an opinion. It's a systematic explanation that can be tested, challenged, and improved. Here's how to build one that actually works."

1Falsifiability

Your theory must make specific predictions that could be proven wrong. If there's no way to test it, it's not science — it's just opinion.

Example: "Democracies never fight each other" is falsifiable. "Democracies are more peaceful" is too vague.

2Concrete Consequences

Spell out exactly what your theory predicts we should observe in the real world. Vague theories can't be tested.

Bad: "Culture matters for development."
Good: "Countries with higher trust levels will have 2% higher GDP growth."

3Consider Alternatives

Always ask: What else could explain this pattern? The best theories beat out competing explanations.

Example: If democracies are peaceful, is it because of democracy, wealth, or something else entirely?

4Select on the Dependent Variable

Don't just study cases where your theory "works." Include cases where the outcome is different to really test your ideas.

Bad: Only studying successful democracies.
Good: Studying both successful and failed democracies.

5Report Uncertainty

Be honest about what you don't know. Good science acknowledges its limitations and areas of uncertainty.

Example: "This relationship holds in Europe, but we're not sure about other regions."

🔍 Research Design Strategies

Maximizing Leverage

The key insight: Get the most explanatory power from the fewest assumptions. This means:

Smart Case Selection

  • Most Similar Systems: Compare cases that are alike in most ways but differ on your key variable
  • Most Different Systems: Compare very different cases that share the same outcome
  • Crucial Cases: Find cases where your theory should definitely work — if it fails here, it's in trouble

Increasing Observations

Even with just one case study, you can increase your observations by:

  • Over time: Look at the same case across different periods
  • Across levels: Examine national, regional, and local patterns
  • Within cases: Find variation within your main case

🛠️ Practical Example: Studying Democratic Transitions

Bad approach: Just study successful transitions to democracy

Better approach: Compare successful transitions with failed attempts

Best approach: Include cases of:

  • Successful democratization
  • Failed democratization attempts
  • Stable authoritarian regimes
  • Cases that moved backward from democracy

🎯 Why This Matters Beyond the Classroom

Critical Thinking in Daily Life

"These aren't just academic concepts. Every day, politicians, journalists, and experts make causal claims. KKV gives you the tools to evaluate whether those claims actually hold up."

🗞️ Evaluating News Claims

Headline: "Study Shows Coffee Reduces Heart Disease Risk"

KKV Questions to Ask:

  • Is this falsifiable? Could we find evidence that would disprove it?
  • What's the counterfactual? How do we know it's the coffee and not something else?
  • Did they consider alternative explanations? Maybe coffee drinkers exercise more?
  • What about selection bias? Did they only study healthy coffee drinkers?

🏛️ Evaluating Policy Arguments

Claim: "Charter schools improve student performance"

KKV Analysis:

  • Causal inference problem: How do we know it's the charter school and not motivated parents?
  • Selection on dependent variable: Are we only looking at successful charter schools?
  • Alternative explanations: Could it be resources, teacher quality, or peer effects?
  • Uncertainty: What don't we know about long-term effects?

Building Better Arguments

Whether you're writing a paper, making a business case, or debating policy, KKV helps you:

  • Make testable claims: Say exactly what evidence would prove you wrong
  • Address counterarguments: Anticipate and respond to alternative explanations
  • Be honest about limits: Acknowledge what your evidence can and can't prove
  • Think systematically: Consider the full range of relevant cases

📝 Study Questions

The Shared Logic:

Because both seek to make inferences from evidence, even though they use different tools. The logic of explanation and testing applies to both styles.

What This Means:

  • Same goal: Both try to move from observations to broader understanding
  • Same challenges: Both face the counterfactual problem and selection bias
  • Same standards: Both need falsifiable theories and transparent methods
  • Different tools: Numbers vs. words, but same underlying reasoning

Example:

A quantitative study might survey 1000 people about voting behavior, while a qualitative study might interview 20 people in depth. Both are trying to understand why people vote the way they do - they just use different approaches to get there.

Simple Definition:

Drawing conclusions that go beyond what is directly observed. It means making claims about patterns, causes, or meanings that aren't immediately visible in the data.

Examples:

  • Description (not inference): "Candidate A received 55% of votes"
  • Inference: "Campaign ads caused higher voter turnout"
  • Description: "Germany has been peaceful since 1945"
  • Inference: "Democracy makes countries less likely to fight wars"

Why It's Central:

Inference is what makes research scientific rather than just descriptive. Anyone can count things or describe events. Science happens when we try to explain why those patterns exist and what they mean for other cases.

The Challenge:

Good inference requires moving carefully from specific observations to general claims, while acknowledging uncertainty and considering alternative explanations.

The Problem:

To prove that X causes Y, we need to observe the exact same situation both with X present and with X absent — the counterfactual. This is literally impossible because we can't rewind time and run the same situation twice.

Why It's Impossible:

  • Time moves forward: We can't replay history with different conditions
  • Everything is connected: Changing one thing affects everything else
  • Unique contexts: No two situations are exactly identical

Example:

To prove democracy causes peace, we'd need to see the exact same Germany — same history, culture, leaders, economy — but somehow not democratic. Since that's impossible, we have to use approximations and make assumptions.

The Workaround:

We get as close as possible by finding similar cases, controlling for differences, and being explicit about our assumptions. Perfect proof is impossible, but we can build strong evidence.

The Core Idea:

Science is defined by how we study, not what we study. The topic doesn't determine whether research is scientific — the method does.

What This Means:

  • Any topic can be scientific: Love, culture, religion, emotions — if studied systematically
  • Method matters most: Clear theories, transparent procedures, acknowledged uncertainty
  • No "soft" vs "hard" subjects: Only rigorous vs non-rigorous approaches

Examples:

Scientific approach to culture: "Countries with higher social trust have 15% lower corruption rates" (testable, specific)

Non-scientific approach: "Culture is really important for understanding politics" (vague, untestable)

Why This Matters:

It breaks down artificial barriers between "quantitative" and "qualitative" topics. Even the most complex human behaviors can be studied scientifically if we use rigorous methods.

Definition:

Specific outcomes we expect if our theory is right. They connect abstract ideas to concrete evidence that can be observed and measured.

Why They're Essential:

  • Make theories testable: Turn vague ideas into specific predictions
  • Enable falsification: Give clear criteria for what would prove the theory wrong
  • Guide research: Tell us what evidence to look for
  • Allow comparison: Let us evaluate competing theories

Example:

Theory: "Democratic institutions reduce conflict"

Observable implications:

  • Democracies should fight fewer wars than non-democracies
  • Democratic transitions should be followed by reduced military spending
  • Countries with stronger democratic institutions should have fewer civil conflicts

The Test:

If we don't find these patterns in the real world, we have reason to doubt or revise our theory. Observable implications are the bridge between abstract thinking and empirical evidence.

The Concept:

Use a small set of clear causes to explain a large amount of variation. Get the most explanatory power from the fewest assumptions.

Why It's Valuable:

  • Efficiency: Explains more with less
  • Clarity: Focuses on what really matters
  • Generalizability: Simple explanations often apply more broadly
  • Testability: Fewer moving parts make theories easier to test

Example:

High leverage: "Economic inequality explains both crime rates and political instability across countries"

Low leverage: "Crime depends on inequality, culture, history, geography, institutions, demographics, and weather patterns"

The Balance:

The goal isn't oversimplification — it's finding the right level of complexity. Good theories are as simple as possible, but no simpler than necessary to explain the phenomena.

Practical Application:

When designing research, ask: "What are the few key factors that really drive the outcomes I care about?" Focus your limited time and resources on testing those crucial relationships.

The Traditional Divide:

  • Quantitative: Numbers, statistics, large samples, generalization
  • Qualitative: Words, case studies, deep context, understanding
  • The Problem: Seen as fundamentally different approaches with different goals

KKV's Bridge:

Both approaches share the same logic of scientific inference — they're trying to make valid causal claims about the world.

Shared Principles:

  • Causal inference: Both face the counterfactual problem
  • Theory testing: Both need falsifiable theories
  • Bias concerns: Both must worry about selection effects
  • Uncertainty: Both should acknowledge limitations

What This Means:

  • Qualitative researchers can use rigorous scientific standards without losing depth
  • Quantitative researchers can learn from qualitative insights about context and process
  • Both approaches can inform and strengthen each other

The Result:

Instead of two separate camps, we get one social science with different tools but shared standards for making reliable knowledge about the social world.

Policy Claim Example:

"Mandatory minimum sentences reduce crime rates"

Applying KKV's Framework:

1. Falsifiability Test:

  • Good: The claim makes a specific, testable prediction
  • Question: What would count as evidence against it? Rising crime despite mandatory minimums?

2. Causal Inference Challenge:

  • Counterfactual problem: What would crime rates be WITHOUT mandatory minimums?
  • Need: Compare similar jurisdictions with and without the policy

3. Alternative Explanations:

  • Economic conditions improving?
  • More police on the streets?
  • Demographic changes?
  • Other criminal justice reforms?

4. Selection Bias Check:

  • Bad: Only studying places where crime fell after implementing policy
  • Better: Include places where crime stayed same or rose

5. Uncertainty Assessment:

  • What don't we know about long-term effects?
  • Does it work for all types of crime?
  • What about unintended consequences?

Better Research Design:

Compare states before/after implementation, control for economic and demographic factors, include states that didn't adopt the policy, and be honest about what the evidence can and can't tell us.
