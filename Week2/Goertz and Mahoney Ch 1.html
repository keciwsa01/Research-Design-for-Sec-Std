Two Cultures of Social Science Research

Interactive Study Guide - Gertz & Mahoney's Revolutionary Framework

📖 Core Claim: Two Distinct Cultures

Goertz and Mahoney's revolutionary argument: Quantitative and qualitative research aren't just different "styles" - they are distinct cultures with their own values, norms, and default tools. This is why misunderstandings across them are so common!

The Book's Goal

Clarify differences to improve communication and enable smart mixing of methods. It's not about declaring a winner - it's about understanding what each culture brings to the table.

🎯 Disagreement with KKV (King, Keohane & Verba)

The traditional "one logic" argument claimed both approaches share the same logic of inference (basically statistical). Goertz & Mahoney disagree:

  • The two cultures rest on different foundations
  • They pursue inference differently
  • Quantitative draws on probability/statistics
  • Qualitative often (implicitly) draws on logic and set theory

🔍 What Each Culture Actually Does

This isn't just "numbers vs words" - it's about fundamentally different approaches to understanding causation:

Quantitative Culture

Cross-case analysis to make inferences about populations

Examples: experiments, statistical models, surveys

Qualitative Culture

Within-case analysis to make inferences about individual cases

Examples: process tracing, case studies, historical analysis

The real distinction: Population vs Case and Statistics vs Logic

🧠 The Logic Behind Qualitative Thinking

Qualitative researchers think about causes in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions:

Necessary Condition

Something that must be present for an outcome to occur. Without it, the outcome cannot happen.

Example: Oxygen is necessary for fire

Sufficient Condition

Something that, if present, guarantees the outcome will happen.

Example: A match plus oxygen plus fuel is sufficient for fire

When qualitative researchers say a factor "prevents" something, they're making a claim about necessary conditions. When they say it "ensures" an outcome, that's the language of sufficiency.

🤔 Why "Two Cultures" (Not One or Many)?

The authors focus on causal inference traditions on both sides (not interpretive approaches). Here's why this framework makes sense:

  • The quant/qual split already structures academic life
  • Different departments, journals, and training institutes
  • ICPSR (quantitative) vs IQMR (qualitative) summer schools
  • It's a useful map of how research actually works

📊 How They Study These Cultures

The authors don't just theorize - they provide evidence by analyzing:

  1. Methods textbooks - What gets taught
  2. Exemplary studies - What gets celebrated
  3. Coded sample of top journal articles - What researchers actually do

This captures typical practice, not just ideals!

🎭 Explicit vs Implicit Methods

A key insight: Quantitative methods are usually explicit and codified in textbooks. Qualitative methods are often implicit, so the authors reconstruct the logic at work.

The Monsieur Jourdan Moment

Like the character who discovers he's been speaking prose his whole life, many qualitative researchers have been using formal logic (necessary/sufficient conditions, Mill's methods) without realizing it!

Seeing the two side-by-side exposes hidden assumptions like symmetry vs asymmetry.

🔬 What's Distinctive About Qualitative Work

The book identifies key features that make qualitative research unique:

Heavy Use of Within-Case Analysis

Especially process tracing to test causal claims by following the causal chain step-by-step within individual cases.

Pervasive Use of Logic and Set Theory

Often unspoken reliance on:

  • Necessary and sufficient conditions
  • Mill's methods (agreement, difference, etc.)
  • Hoop and smoking-gun tests
  • QCA (Qualitative Comparative Analysis)

🚀 The Bottom Line & Path Forward

The key insight: Neither culture is "better." Each suits different research goals!

The Right Question

Instead of asking "Which is better?", ask: "What question am I trying to answer?"

  • Estimating average policy effects across a country? → Quantitative tools
  • Explaining why one revolution succeeded while another failed? → Qualitative tools

Many projects benefit from mixed-methods - but mixing works best when we:

  1. First recognize real differences
  2. Understand the strengths/limits each brings
  3. Learn to communicate across the cultural divide
  4. Create "bilingual scholars" fluent in both languages

1) What do Goertz & Mahoney mean by calling quantitative and qualitative research "two cultures," and why isn't the difference just "numbers vs. words"? ▼

Goertz and Mahoney use "two cultures" to signal that quantitative and qualitative research are not merely two ways of writing up results, but two coherent systems of values, norms, and default practices. Inside each culture, people share assumptions about good questions to ask, legitimate forms of evidence, and how to argue causally; across cultures, those assumptions often clash—leading to miscommunication and frustration.

Crucially, they reject the popular claim (associated with King, Keohane, and Verba) that both traditions share one underlying statistical logic and differ only in style. Instead, they argue the foundations diverge:

  • Quantitative work is rooted in probability and inferential statistics and typically reasons across cases to draw conclusions about populations
  • Qualitative work is rooted (often implicitly) in logic and set theory and typically reasons within cases to make inferences about specific cases and the causal processes inside them

So the real contrast isn't "numbers vs. words" but cross-case statistical inference vs. within-case logical/set-theoretic reasoning, each suited to different research goals. Mixed-methods can be powerful, they add, but only after we recognize these real differences and use each culture where it's strongest.

2) How do the authors plan to characterize and compare the two cultures, and why do they focus on "typical practices" rather than "best practices"? ▼

They triangulate using three sources:

  1. Methods texts to surface each culture's official rules and rationales (e.g., econometrics and potential-outcomes texts for quant; Sartori, George & Bennett, Collier, and Ragin for qual)
  2. Exemplary studies to show what those rules look like in real research on substantive topics
  3. Coded journal articles from top outlets in political science and sociology to see what scholars actually do in practice

This design lets them distinguish between explicit practices (more common in quantitative work because textbooks codify steps and assumptions) and implicit practices (more common in qualitative work, where researchers often deploy logic—like necessary and sufficient conditions—without always formalizing it).

They emphasize typical practices because debates over "best" practices are endless and contested; by sticking to what appears in top journals, they capture the real, prevailing habits that define each culture. They also note some "possible practices"—things a culture could do with its tools but rarely does—because tools make certain moves natural (e.g., averages and symmetric effects in quant; asymmetry, necessity, sufficiency in qual) and others awkward.

3) According to the authors, what is distinctive about qualitative research in this book, and how does it differ from interpretive approaches? ▼

Within the scope of this book, the hallmark of qualitative research is within-case analysis, especially process tracing—using detailed, case-specific evidence (sometimes "smoking guns") to test whether a hypothesized cause actually operated through the expected steps inside a case.

Alongside this, qualitative work leans heavily—often implicitly—on logic and set theory, particularly necessary and sufficient conditions. These ideas underpin classic tools like:

  • Mill's methods
  • Hoop tests and smoking-gun tests in process tracing
  • Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA)

That package—within-case process evidence + logical/set-theoretic reasoning—distinguishes qualitative causal inference from the quantitative culture's cross-case statistical estimation.

The authors explicitly bracket interpretive approaches (which prioritize meaning and power over estimating causal effects) to keep the comparison focused on two causal-inference traditions. In short, their "qualitative" is the causal, case-oriented tradition, not the interpretive one; it's distinct because it treats causation through case mechanisms and logical relations rather than population-level averages.
