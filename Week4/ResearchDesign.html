What is Research Design?

A deliberate plan that outlines how a research question will be answered. It specifies the type of information to be used and dictates how conclusions can be generalized.

Key Point: The selection of a design is strategic, based on the research question and available data.

üìä Unit of Analysis

The general grouping of what is being studied (individuals, states, countries, policies)

Example: In a study of U.S. states, the unit is "states"

üéØ Research Population

The complete set of all possible observations within a unit of analysis that share particular characteristics

Example: If studying only southern states, population becomes "southern states"

üìã II. Classification of Research Designs

1. Quantitative vs. Qualitative

üìà Quantitative ("Large-n")

Uses numerical measures for many observations to identify statistical correlations

Example: Tausanovitch & Warshaw (2014) analyzed 428-1,619 U.S. cities, finding liberal populations correlate with liberal policies and higher taxes.

üìù Qualitative (Case Study)

In-depth analysis of few cases to understand how and why events unfolded

Example: Moore (2015) studied VA's "entrepreneurial bureaucrats" who secured agency survival through private partnerships despite reputation issues.

2. Experimental vs. Observational

üß™ Experimental

Researcher controls treatment assignment to identify precise causal mechanisms

Example: Hock et al. (2012) randomly called 108 business owners about civic meetings. Attendance: 8.3% (treatment) vs 4.7% (control).

üëÅÔ∏è Observational

No manipulation; identifies patterns in existing data as they occur naturally

Example: Dube et al. (2013) found 60% increase in gun homicides in Mexican border areas after U.S. assault weapons ban expired.

3. Comparative Designs

üîÑ Most Similar Systems

Similar cases; isolate varying factor to explain different outcomes

Example: Nepstad (2013) compared Egypt, Syria, Bahrain (similar contexts) to explain different military defection patterns in Arab Spring.

üåç Most Different Systems

Different cases with same outcome; find common causal factor

Example: Skocpol (1979) compared French, Russian, Chinese revolutions (very different contexts) to identify common revolutionary causes.

üîÄ Mixed-Methods Designs

Combines different approaches for more complete answers to complex questions

Example: Wallace et al. (2014) used quantitative survey analysis (large protests decreased Latino political efficacy, small ones increased it) + 100+ qualitative interviews (revealed larger protests had divisive messaging, smaller ones had unified frames).

üéØ III. Sampling and Generalization

Core Concept

Most studies use a sample (smaller group) to make inferences about a larger population through generalization.

‚ö†Ô∏è Sample Bias: When a sample doesn't adequately reflect its population, leading to faulty inferences.

üìä Probability Samples

Based on randomization - every observation has equal, independent chance of selection

Key Statistical Concepts

  • Population Parameter: True characteristic of entire population (often unknown)
  • Sample Statistic: Information from sample used to estimate parameter
  • Sampling Error: Expected difference between statistic and true parameter
  • Margin of Error: Measure of estimate precision
  • Confidence Interval: Range expected to contain true parameter (e.g., 52%-56%)
  • Confidence Level: Probability method will work (typically 95%)
Simple Random Sample

Equal chance for all observations

Systematic Sample

Every nth observation from random start

Stratified Random Sample

Random selection within predetermined categories (e.g., World Values Survey)

Cluster Sample

Random clusters, then observations within clusters

üé≤ Nonprobability Samples

Not based on randomization - unknown selection probability, higher bias risk

Quota Sample

Deliberate selection to mirror population characteristics (e.g., 6 legislators from 60% majority party)

Convenience Sample

Cases with easiest access (language, geography)

Purposive Sample

Cases believed to yield strongest results (e.g., most intense ethnic conflicts)

Snowball Sample

Start with one case, use it to identify others

Standard: 1,000 randomly selected individuals typically produces ¬±3% margin of error at 95% confidence level

üìö Key Terms & Definitions

Research Design

Deliberate plan outlining how a research question will be answered, specifying information type and generalization methods.

Unit of Analysis

The general grouping of what is being studied (individuals, states, countries, policies).

Sample Bias

When a sample doesn't adequately reflect its population, leading to faulty inferences.

Confidence Interval

Range of values expected to contain the true population parameter (e.g., 52%-56% from 54% ¬± 2%).

Treatment Group

In experiments, the group that receives the independent variable manipulation.

Large-n Study

Quantitative research using numerical measures for many observations to identify statistical correlations.

Generalization

The ability to apply sample conclusions to observations not formally studied in the research population.

Control Group

In experiments, the group that does not receive the treatment, used for comparison.

Margin of Error

A measure of the precision of an estimate, typically ¬±3% for 1,000-person samples at 95% confidence.

Population Parameter

True characteristic of entire population (often unknown), estimated using sample statistics.

Case Study

Qualitative research involving in-depth, descriptive investigation of one or more cases.

Stratified Sample

Population divided into categories (strata), then random selection from each stratum proportionally.
