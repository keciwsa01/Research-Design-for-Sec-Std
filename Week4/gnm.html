üî¨ Causal Models and Selection Logic

üìä Statistical Model

Objective:

Estimate average treatment effect of X on Y across population

Logic:

Assumes additive, linear relationship. How does change in X correlate with change in Y?

Optimal Strategy:

Random selection from larger population

Ensures representativeness and unbiased correlation estimates

üéØ Set-Theoretic Model

Objective:

Evaluate necessary and/or sufficient conditions

Logic:

Not about "average" effects but logical dependencies

Optimal Strategy:

Purposive selection of specific case types

Strategic targeting for maximum analytical leverage

(X ‚Üí Y)		 X always produces Y Select cases where X=1

üìà The Geddes Example: Labor Repression & Economic Growth

üî¨ Competing Interpretations of the Same Data

Geddes (1991, 2003) analyzed the relationship between labor repression and economic growth, sparking debate about case selection methods.

üìä Geddes's Statistical Argument

Criticism: Scholars who select only high-growth East Asian countries (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) are committing selection bias.

Evidence: Bivariate analysis of larger sample shows R¬≤ = .023 (virtually no linear relationship)

Conclusion: Labor repression is not a significant predictor of growth

üéØ Set-Theoretic Counter-Argument

Key Evidence: "Empty space" in upper-left quadrant of scatterplot

Pattern: No cases of high growth + low repression

Conclusion: Strong support for labor repression as necessary condition for high growth

üîë Key Insight

The same data supports different conclusions depending on whether you're testing statistical correlations or set-theoretic relationships. Selecting high-growth cases to test necessity is perfectly valid and efficient.

üö® Cell (0,1) - Critical for Necessity

Outcome present, cause absent. Most critical for testing necessary conditions. One case here falsifies "X necessary for Y"

üö® Cell (1,0) - Critical for Sufficiency

Cause present, outcome absent. Most critical for testing sufficient conditions. One case here disproves "X sufficient for Y"

‚ûñ Cell (0,0) - Limited Leverage

üõ†Ô∏è Key Case Selection Strategies

üé≤ 1. Random Selection

Goal

Representative sample of larger population

Primary Use

Standard strategy in quantitative research for statistical models

Limitation

Inefficient for small-N studies; may miss crucial cases from cells (0,1) or (1,0)

‚≠ê 2. Substantively Important Cases

Goal

Explain specific cases of major theoretical/historical interest

Primary Use

Common in qualitative research (e.g., French Revolution)

Logic

"Good theories must explain substantively important cases"

üìä 3. Selection Based on Large-N Statistical Models

Goal

Use case studies to supplement and unpack statistical findings

Primary Use

Multi-method research combining quantitative and qualitative approaches

Typical Cases

Cases on or near regression line, representing average relationship

Deviant/Off-Line Cases

Cases with large residuals poorly explained by statistical model

Limitation: Remains anchored to statistical logic; may not effectively discover set-theoretic relationships not captured by linear regression

üéØ Conclusion: Aligning Method with Research Goals

üîë Key Principle

There is no universally superior case selection strategy. The choice must align with research goals and causal arguments.

üìä For Statistical Inference

Goal: Estimate average causal effects within population

Method: Random selection is ideal

üéØ For Set-Theoretic Analysis

Goal: Test necessary/sufficient conditions or conduct process tracing

Method: Purposive selection is required

‚úÖ Methodological Legitimacy

Qualitative research rightfully uses purposive selection because its objectives differ from quantitative research. By strategically targeting specific cells in the 2x2 causal table, qualitative researchers maximize analytical leverage for evaluating set-theoretic hypotheses. This is not a methodological flaw but a rigorous procedure for theory building and testing.
