Types of Theories

Theories can be classified based on how they predict outcomes and the certainty of their predictions.

üé≤ Probabilistic Theories

Suggest that outcomes are more or less likely based on certain variables. Most social science theories fall into this category.

‚öôÔ∏è Deterministic Theories

Suggest that an outcome is the only possible result from a particular cause. Less common in social sciences.

üí° Key Insight: Social science theories are usually probabilistic because human behavior and social phenomena involve multiple complex factors that make outcomes likely rather than certain.

Establishing Causality

To prove that one variable causes another, researchers must satisfy three critical requirements.

Empirical Correlation

The observed cause and the observed effect must be empirically correlated - they must vary together in a measurable way.

Temporal Precedence

The observed cause must precede the observed effect in time - the cause must happen before the effect.

No Alternative Explanation

The variation between two variables cannot be explained away by a third variable that actually caused the variation in both.

Competing Theories

In social science, multiple theories often attempt to explain the same phenomenon. Researchers must determine which theory provides the best explanation.

üéØ Explanatory Power

Researchers test which theory has the most explanatory power - the ability to explain an outcome most effectively and comprehensively.

From Theory to Hypotheses

Theories provide general explanations, but hypotheses make specific, testable predictions about relationships between variables.

üî¨ Research Hypothesis

Specifies the expected relationship between variables and is testable through empirical research.

‚ùå Null Hypothesis

States that there is no relationship between the independent and dependent variables.

Essential Components of a Hypothesis

A well-formed hypothesis should include:

  • A value of the independent variable - the factor you think causes change
  • The expected value of the dependent variable - the outcome you expect to observe
  • A comparison group - a baseline for measuring the effect

Purpose of Literature Review

Literature reviews serve as the foundation for developing strong theories and understanding existing research.

üìà Trace Evolution

To trace the evolution of ideas pertaining to a research topic over time and understand how thinking has developed.

üí° Generate Ideas

To generate ideas about why there is variation in a dependent variable and identify potential explanatory factors.

üìö Key Value: The literature contains a record of how scholars have thought about political outcomes and offers valuable insights into why variation exists. Researchers use these ideas to shape their own theories and refine established ones.
