üéØ Introduction: The Challenge of Causation ‚ñº

Imagine trying to understand why World War I erupted in 1914. Unlike laboratory scientists who can run controlled experiments, political scientists and historians face a fundamental challenge: we cannot rerun history. We cannot put major historical events "in a Petri dish" to test different variables.

When analysts try to assess causation for major, unique, and consequential events‚Äîrevolutions, democratic collapses, or the start of wars‚Äîthey inevitably must look at what didn't happen. This guide explores the necessary and often hidden role of counterfactual conditionals in serious political inquiry.

Counterfactual Conditional

A claim about events that didn't actually occur, taking the form: "If it had been the case that C had happened (or not happened), then it would have been the case that E would have resulted (or not resulted)." It's a causal hypothesis framed as a hypothetical world‚Äîa "what if?"

üåê The Ubiquity of Counterfactual Reasoning ‚ñº

Counterfactual reasoning appears as the scaffolding under almost every major claim about history or politics. Consider these influential examples:

üìö Case Study: Barrington Moore's Democratic Development

Barrington Moore argued that "without the prior democratic modernization of England, the reactionary methods adopted in Germany and Japan would scarcely have been possible." His entire comparative thesis rests on the counterfactual idea: What if Germany and Japan had followed England's democratic path?

üìö Case Study: Kenneth Waltz on Bipolarity

Kenneth Waltz argued that nuclear weapons did not cause post-WWII bipolarity. He hypothesized that "had the atom never been split, the US and Soviet Union would still have far surpassed everyone else in military and economic strength." By imagining a non-nuclear world with the same bipolar result, he argued against the causal importance of nuclear weapons.

üîÑ Two Strategies for Establishing Causation ‚ñº

Since controlled experiments are impossible for major historical events, analysts use two main strategies to determine if C caused E:

üîÑ Two Research Strategies

üìù Explicit Counterfactual Strategy

Method: Direct "what if" thinking

Approach: Imagine C was absent in your specific case

Question: "Would E still have happened?"

Best for: Small-N research, unique events

üìä Actual Case Strategy

Method: Statistical comparison

Approach: Find real cases where C was absent

Question: "Are C and E associated across cases?"

Best for: Large-N research, multiple cases

üí° Key Insight

Both small-N and large-N research strategies rely on counterfactuals‚Äîthe difference is how explicitly they engage with counterfactual assumptions.

The Explicit Counterfactual Strategy

This approach directly imagines that C was absent in the specific case being studied and asks: "Would E have still happened in that hypothetical situation?" This is the direct "what if" method commonly used in small-N research.

The Actual Case Strategy

This approach looks for other real-world examples that were similar in important ways but where cause C was absent. Analysts then check if C and E are associated across these real cases. This includes statistical studies and rigorous comparative history using methods like regression analysis or Mill's methods.

Example: You have one case (Weimar's collapse) but 20 potential causes: economic crisis, Versailles Treaty, political infighting, cultural factors, etc. This creates "negative degrees of freedom"‚Äîtoo many variables, not enough cases to draw statistically valid causal conclusions.

The Hidden Counterfactual in Statistical Analysis

Even massive statistical studies rely on a critical, often unstated counterfactual assumption. When you accept that a regression coefficient represents causation (not just correlation), you're implicitly accepting a counterfactual claim about the error term.

Error Term Assumption

The assumption that if individual cases had possessed different values on your independent variables, all the other unmeasured, unspecified factors influencing the outcome would not have differed systematically just because the independent variable changed.

Example: If you're studying economic inequality's effect on civil unrest across 100 countries and find correlation, claiming causation requires believing that if Country X suddenly became more economically equal, its specific history, culture, and political quirks (the unmeasured "error term") would remain stable enough that your model still correctly predicts reduced unrest.

The Cotenability Principle

The most critical methodological concept for evaluating counterfactual claims is cotenability.

Cotenability

A counterfactual thought experiment is only judged plausible if the counterfactual assertion itself is coatable (compatible) with the facts and theories used to draw the conclusion. It asks: If the antecedent had been true, would anything else necessarily have been different in a way that would undermine your conclusion?

Cotenability tests the stability of your hypothetical world. Does changing factor C force you to change factors X, Y, and Z too? You want your "what if" to require the minimum possible rewriting of history.

‚ùå Low Cotenability

üìö Case Study: Sagan's Critique of Van Evera

Scott Sagan challenged Van Evera's WWI counterfactual by arguing that purely defensive doctrines might create different strategic problems. Armies that can only defend themselves cannot offer credible extended deterrence to protect smaller allies. This might actually increase chances for smaller, localized wars that gradually shift the balance of power, eventually leading to major conflict through a different path.

The Debate: Which hypothetical scenario requires fewer unrealistic changes? The argument shifts to the relative cotenability of different imagined worlds.

Applications in Comparative Politics

Small-N case studies in comparative politics heavily rely on assigning causal weight to specific choices made by leaders, using counterfactual reasoning to isolate agency from structural factors.

üìö Case Study: Stepan's Analysis of Brazil's 1964 Coup

Argument: Alfred Stepan argued that macro-political factors (economic problems, social tensions) didn't make the coup inevitable. A "small margin of maneuverability" remained.

Causal Weight: Assigned to President Goulart's specific choices‚Äîproposing major reforms that antagonized powerful groups and being too lenient with a naval mutiny.

Counterfactual Justification: Had Goulart chosen different strategies or handled the mutiny differently, a coup might not have occurred. Historical evidence shows military leadership was divided and reluctant to intervene before Goulart's polarizing moves.

Function: Highlights human agency and contingency against seemingly overwhelming structural pressures.

The Multicollinearity Challenge

Comparative work faces the multicollinearity challenge when several potential causes always appear together. If conditions A, B, C, D, and E all occur in every case of successful democracy and are absent in non-democratic cases, how do you determine which factors actually matter?

Solution: Run explicit counterfactual arguments for each factor. Mentally "unbundle" the correlated variables by asking: "Would democracy still emerge in a world with A, B, C, and E, but without D?"

Logical Traps: The Cleopatra's Nose Problem

Counterfactual reasoning faces important logical pitfalls. The most famous is the Cleopatra's Nose Problem.

The Problem: If Cleopatra's nose had been shorter and less beautiful by Roman standards, maybe Mark Antony wouldn't have been smitten. Maybe he would have stayed focused on Rome. Maybe the Roman Republic wouldn't have fallen the way it did. Maybe the entire course of Western history changes.

While this satisfies the simple "if not A, then not B" conditional test, do we really want to call the length of Cleopatra's nose a significant cause of the Roman Republic's fall? It feels trivial‚Äîa proximate trigger at best, not a deep cause.

The Lesson: A true cause needs to do more than satisfy the simple counterfactual test. It needs to generalize or significantly select out that particular outcome in a meaningful way. It needs theoretical weight and must pass the cotenability test.

Methodological Implications and Bias

The cotenability principle reveals an important methodological bias: it's generally easier to plausibly imagine a single political leader making a different choice while keeping the overall structure the same, compared to imagining vast structural conditions being different.

High Cotenability: "What if Goulart hadn't been lenient on the mutiny?" (minimal rewrite of 1964 Brazil)
Low Cotenability: "What if Brazil hadn't experienced import substitution industrialization?" (requires changing almost everything about 20th century Brazilian history)

Implication: We might systematically give greater analytical weight to causal claims based on highly coatable counterfactuals‚Äîoften claims about specific choices, actions, and policies‚Äîbecause they are more methodologically defensible.

Provocative Question: Which counterfactual is easier to defend as plausible: a world where one specific contingent choice was made differently, or a world where a vast structural condition like the Industrial Revolution simply didn't exist?

üìã Chapter Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Counterfactuals are unavoidable: Both small-N and large-N research rely on counterfactual reasoning to establish causation.
  • Two main strategies: Explicit counterfactual (direct "what if") and actual case comparison (statistical analysis with hidden counterfactual assumptions).
  • Justification requires two pillars: General theoretical principles and specific historical knowledge.
  • Cotenability is crucial: Strong counterfactuals require minimal changes to the historical context.
  • Methodological bias exists: Our tools may be better at detecting agency than structural causation.
  • Practical prescription: Minimize changes required by your counterfactual scenario to strengthen causal inference.

üìñ Key Terms Glossary

Counterfactual Conditional
A claim about events that didn't occur, framed as "if...then" statements to test causal hypotheses.
Cotenability
The principle that counterfactuals should require minimal changes to historical context to be methodologically sound.
Small-N Research
Studies examining few cases (often just one) with many potential explanatory variables.
Large-N Research
Statistical studies examining many cases to identify patterns and correlations.
Error Term
In statistical analysis, represents all unmeasured factors that might influence the outcome.
Cult of the Offensive
Pre-WWI military doctrine believing that attacking first provides decisive advantages in warfare.
Multicollinearity
When multiple potential causes always appear together, making it difficult to isolate individual causal effects.
Negative Degrees of Freedom
Having more potential explanatory variables than cases to study, preventing statistical analysis.
Explicit Counterfactual Strategy
Directly imagining alternative scenarios to test causal claims through "what if" reasoning.
Actual Case Strategy
Comparing real-world cases where potential causes vary to identify causal relationships.
